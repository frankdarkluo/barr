# Research Log — 2026-04-05

## Summary
**Key finding:** Bias risk is not only elevated in transition-bearing trajectories overall, but increases sharply with transition recurrence, establishing repeated entry into transition states as a robust and consistent fairness risk marker.

---

## Purpose
This session pursued two goals:
1. Quantify the relationship between transition-token recurrence (occurrences of `Wait`, `Hmm`, `Alternatively`) and eventual biased outcomes across all four Qwen3-8B variants on the BBQ ambiguous split.
2. Finalize the four-phase experimental roadmap for BARR and execute Phase A (Experiment 1: transition-order hazard analysis) on existing JSONL data.

---

## Protocol
- **Dataset:** BBQ (9 categories), ambiguous split only, aggregated across 5 random seeds (0, 1, 2, 42, 1999); N = 313,661 valid samples.
- **Models:** Qwen3-8B in four quantization variants — BF16, AWQ-Int4, FP8, GPTQ-Int4.
- **Procedure:**
  1. Refactored and consolidated the transition-token extraction and visualization pipeline (`transition_stats_figures.py`) to produce 8 paper-ready PDF figures with parallel loading (8 workers, ~58 s end-to-end).
  2. Designed the four-phase BARR experimental roadmap: Exp 1 (transition-order hazard), Exp 2 (hidden-state window analysis), Exp 3 (answer-logit margin dynamics), Exp 4 (intervention timing ablation).
  3. Executed **Phase A — Experiment 1** (`exp1_hazard_by_order.py`) on existing data, computing two complementary metrics: the cumulative at-risk hazard $P(\text{biased} \mid K \ge k)$ and the exact-count bias rate $P(\text{biased} \mid K = k)$.
  4. Identified `religion` and `age` as the Phase B pilot categories based on per-category biased-positive counts (198 and 252 positives respectively across 5 seeds in BF16).

---

## Results

**Exp 1A — Exact Transition Count vs. Biased Rate**

| Model | K=0 | K=1 | K=2 | K=3 | K=4+ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Qwen3-8B (BF16) | 0.52% | 0.42% | 0.55% | **2.20%** | **9.89%** |
| Qwen3-8B (FP8) | 0.34% | 0.53% | 0.70% | **2.87%** | **9.86%** |
| Qwen3-8B (AWQ-Int4) | 0.21% | 0.41% | 0.84% | **2.24%** | **9.77%** |
| Qwen3-8B (GPTQ-Int4) | 0.52% | 0.71% | 1.52% | **5.16%** | **13.92%** |

**Exp 1B — Incremental Hazard ΔH(k)** = $P(\text{biased} \mid K \ge k) - P(\text{biased} \mid K \ge k{-}1)$

| Model | ΔH(k≥2) | ΔH(k≥3) | ΔH(k≥4) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Qwen3-8B (BF16) | +1.13% | **+3.79%** | +3.47% |
| Qwen3-8B (FP8) | +1.34% | **+3.75%** | +2.95% |
| Qwen3-8B (AWQ-Int4) | +1.56% | **+3.09%** | +3.14% |
| Qwen3-8B (GPTQ-Int4) | +2.82% | **+4.98%** | +3.27% |

**Figures**
- `figures/exp1/exp1_hazard_rate.pdf` — At-risk hazard rate as a function of transition order $k$; all four models show a monotonically increasing curve.
- `figures/exp1/exp1_exact_count_risk.pdf` — Biased rate by exact transition count; the critical inflection at $K \ge 3$ is visually clear, with 4–20× risk amplification relative to $K \le 2$.

---

## Observations
1. **Near-monotone risk accumulation.** The exact-count biased rate increases consistently from $K=0$ through $K=4+$ across all four models, with the Safe Zone ($K \le 2$, ~0.2–0.8%) giving way to a sharply elevated Danger Zone at $K \ge 3$ (2–6%) and $K \ge 4$ (10–14%).
2. **Trajectory-level risk recurrence.** The at-risk hazard analysis confirms that the signal is not driven solely by aggregate path quality: among paths that have already reached transition $k$, the biased rate continues to rise with each subsequent $k$. Risk accumulates as trajectories repeatedly re-enter transition states.
3. **GPTQ-Int4 shows the strongest degradation.** This variant exhibits ΔH(k≥3) = +4.98%, the largest incremental jump, suggesting quantization may amplify transition-recurrence risk.

---

## Hypothesis
- **[High confidence]** Repeated entry into transition states constitutes a robust, model-independent marker of vulnerable reasoning paths, and is more strongly predictive of eventual bias than the identity of any individual transition token.
- **[High confidence — framing implication]** Experiment 1 enables a framing shift for the EMNLP submission: rather than a *token-identity* claim (e.g., "`Alternatively` is risky"), the evidence now supports a cleaner *transition-recurrence* claim — it is the accumulation of transition events itself that is the most stable and generalizable risk signal.

---

## Limitations
Experiment 1 establishes *where* risk concentrates (high-$K$ paths) and provides a first-order clue about *when* it rises ($k=2$ and $k=3$ are the most informative transition orders). However, the analysis remains trajectory-level and correlational. It does not identify the best local intervention point, nor does it constitute a causal test. These questions are addressed in Experiments 2 and 3.
